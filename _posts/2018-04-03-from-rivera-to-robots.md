*From Rivera to Robots: Using art to explore our conversations about automation*

## I. The Totems of American Industry

In 1932, during the depths of the Great Depression, Mexican artist Diego Rivera began painting a mural to fill the walls of an interior courtyard at the Detroit Institute of Arts. Edsel Ford---Rivera's patron and the son of automaker Henry Ford---asked that he capture the spirit of Detroit's economy.

Rivera's twenty-seven panel mural depicts Detroit's industries and their contributions to a broader project---that of modernization, and growth by our society and race. Industrial scenes span both of the long walls of the court; panels above, and at the adjacent ends of the court, depict nature, agriculture, and reproduction. Former DIA director Graham Beal writes this contrast "sets up the series of extraordinary dualities which are the essence of the Rivera mural as a whole."[^1] The principal duality is between man and machine, which Rivera observed across several months in Detroit's sprawling automotive factories, chemical plants, and pharmaceutical labs. He was inspired by Detroit's factories and products, but also saw evil in what machines might allow humans to do to each other. Panels depict children receiving vaccines, but also chemical weapons and warplanes.

The industry panels, and the scenes below, portray work at Ford's River Rouge plant. In the bottom corner of one wall, workers are waiting in an orderly line to begin their shifts; and at the other end of the opposite wall, they're seen leaving. In between, they work in the plant's coking oven and steel furnace, operate a large metal stamping machine, weld exterior panels, build engines, and assemble chassis. Below, apprentices examine car engines and study books. Machines and tools are ubiquitous. One art historian describes the machines as "godlike and mysterious, with enormous totemic power."[^2] There are workers everywhere---either helping each other, working alone, or in teams of four or five alongside an assembly line. They are humanized by their facial expressions, by their varied clothes, and by the work they are performing.

Despite Rivera's growing stature in the country---the New York MoMA had held a retrospective of his work in 1931---the murals opened with controversy. The Detroit *Daily* *News* described them as coarse and vulgar and without meaning.[^3] Others asked why the DIA had hired a Mexican artist when millions of Americans were jobless. Attempting to end the controversy, Edsel Ford announced, "In the years to come, they \[the murals\] will be ranked among the truly great art treasures of America."[^4] Since then, artists, historians, and the public have agreed with Ford's early proclamation. Rivera's style and methods would influence hundreds of murals painted in post offices, courthouses, and libraries throughout the country during the New Deal. Historians, and even Rivera himself, have said it is his best work. And in 2014, while Detroit teetered through municipal bankruptcy proceedings that threatened the existence of the DIA, the murals became a National Historic Landmark.[^5]

Shortly after the Murals opened to the public, Edsel Ford said, "I admire Rivera's spirit. I really believe he was trying to express his idea of the spirit of Detroit."[^6] That belief continues today; the DIA catalog describes the murals as "depict\[ing\] industry and technology as the indigenous culture of Detroit."[^7] Although Rivera painted scenes from an unusually large, economically integrated, and innovative factory, the murals expressed a larger idea: that machines enabled industrial workers to transform natural wealth into products and projects that would modernize society, and make future generations richer and healthier.

## II. Then vs. Now: An Unmistakable Difference

The factories Rivera observed have common denominators with many factories today. Both feature Henry Ford's assembly line. Both have relied on hard working laborers, who clock in, perform repetitious tasks, and clock out each day. Both have been a source of prosperity for thousands of employees and their families.

While there are these similarities, as you might expect, there are many more differences between Rivera's Industry Murals and factories and workplaces today. Only men work in Rivera's industrial scenes, although there are a few women depicted in office jobs, presumably as typists and clerks; today, women comprise 47% of our workforce.[^8] Business practices have also changed: The parts and inputs, like steel, that American manufacturers use to make finished products now come from across the world, instead of local or internal workshops. Throughout the murals, workers are subjected to heat, fumes, and flames; many are clearly physically suffering from the industrial work, and have bluish faces. Rivera even painted himself alongside these unprotected and exposed workers, who he saw as being sacrificed by society.[^9] Regulations, management practices, and innovations have since made factories cleaner and safer.

The starkest contrast relates to how workers interact with machines today. Rivera toured plants throughout Detroit that each had thousands of employees, even during the Great Depression. At River Rouge, he saw a plant with thirty thousand men, aided by machines at every step of the production process.[^10] He did not encounter automated work---which didn't begin in the U.S. until 1961.[^11]

Today, Rivera's oversized, worker-operated machines have been replaced by computerized, delicate robots. At the center of the courtyard's North Wall, three workers are pushing and pulling a cart loaded with engine blocks, as workers behind them use drills and other tools to build engines; today, a single industrial robot positioned alongside an assembly line once full of workers would perform a similar task almost effortlessly---while emitting nothing but a faint whirling sound.

Manufacturers like Ford still employ thousands of workers, but far fewer than what Rivera saw and painted in 1932. River Rouge has 6,000 employees today, just five percent of its high-water mark during the Second World War.[^12] Even if supply chain restructuring and outsourcing can explain part of the decline in employment at River Rouge, industrial robots have displaced thousands of Ford workers. Yes, manufacturing executives have spent the past four decades chasing cheaper, non-unionized labor within the U.S., typically found in southern and southwestern states. Industrial robots, however, have made it possible for manufacturers across the country to produce record amounts with fewer and fewer workers. Economists Daron Acemoglu and Pascual Restrepo recently studied this displacement effect. Using nearly thirty years of labor market and automation data, they found that in the areas most exposed to automated production---including Detroit---an additional robot replaced six workers. [^13]

Thousands of companies have made similar decisions as Ford: There are already more than 1.5 million robots in factories across the country.[^14] They mold, shape, paint, and finish parts, and assemble and box finished products. Many workers no longer have to strain to perform routine tasks, like lifting a part or fastening a screw. Instead, workers monitor tablet screens and occasionally assist machines. Automation has displaced millions of American workers, but has also made work safer and easier for those who have kept their jobs.

## III. Rivera's Racial Idealism

Just as writers and researchers choose to emphasize one point over others when telling a story, or when building an argument, Rivera balanced what he observed while living and working in Detroit with his experiences, prior work, and political beliefs. Despite spending several months in Ford's plants sketching and researching working conditions and industrial processes, Rivera painted scenes that are different from photographs of Detroit's factories in the 1920s and 1930s. He had to compress, rearrange, and omit areas and industrial processes to fit the scenes into mural panels. He chose to paint an earlier version of the large metal stamping machine, which reminded him of the Aztec goddess Coatlicue.[^15]

The most significant departure from reality, however, is Rivera's decision to paint an integrated factory floor in which blacks, whites, and Hispanics performed similar tasks, often together. It was his idealized vision. In 1930, African-Americans made up just four percent of automobile workers, although Ford alone employed half of the total. By 1937, five years after the murals were finished, Ford had 11,000 black workers---12 percent of its workforce. Nearly all worked at River Rouge, in the most undesirable, unskilled, and dangerous jobs. In 1943, Howard University economist Lloyd Bailer obtained occupational and worker demographic data from Detroit's automakers, which he used in an article published in the *Journal of Political Economy.*[^16]

The data tell a starkly different story of the conditions for African-American workers than the integrated scenes in Rivera's murals. Three-quarters of black workers held unskilled positions, in contrast to only a quarter of white workers. One in two white workers held skilled or white-collar positions, compared to one in eight black workers. Few blacks, if any, were in white-collar management roles. (One survey from 1940 found there were only six black managers, out of more than twenty-three thousand black autoworkers.) Bailer writes, "In brief, Negroes were not only concentrated in occupations requiring less skill but were also attached to the worst jobs within each occupational classification. The sole exception to this general characterization was the Ford \[River\] Rouge plant---though here, as described below, the difference was largely one of degree."[^17]

Rivera may have seen some integrated areas of River Rouge, which by 1937 had black workers in every production and nonproduction division. However Ford, as Bailer indicates, was not representative of the auto industry, or of other large employers throughout Detroit and the country. Integration wouldn't begin in many factories until a decade later, when wartime labor shortages forced plant managers to fill some positions with African-Americans. Non-white workers unquestionably gained greater opportunities during and after the Second World War, but historians including Thomas Sugrue have questioned whether Rivera's fully integrated and equal workplaces have ever been true in Detroit's factories.[^18]

As an artist, Rivera concealed racial inequalities---in social, economic, and political life in the 1930s---by painting integrated and equal factories. Eighty-five years later, a similar deception underpins our conversations about automation: We're ignoring durable, widespread, and economically meaningful inequalities when talking about the likely consequences of automation.

## IV. Our Incomplete Conversations

Last year, the *New Yorker* writer Sheelah Kolhatkar wrote that the history of another Michigan company---not Ford but office furniture maker Steelcase---is "the history of manufacturing in America." [^19] Since the 1990s, Steelcase has laid off nearly eighty percent of its workforce, over 8,000 employees, and shut down more than a dozen plants---all while making and selling more desks and chairs and waste baskets across the world. Steelcase executives have moved thousands of factory jobs from plants in Western Michigan to locations abroad, but the company's shrinking employment rolls can ultimately be traced to its use of industrial robots.

Although Kolhatkar's thesis is about the future of factories---the article is titled "Welcoming Our New Robot Overlords"---Kolhatkar captures how we discuss, write, and think about the effects of automation and other disruptive technologies. Her article, and the public discourse surrounding automation, can be defined by a duality---of fear and inspiration---similar to that which preoccupied Rivera's mind and hand in the early 1930s. Rivera was hopeful that factories full of machines would enrich the working class, yet he understood they could support war and often endangered workers. Today, citizens and politicians anticipate a future---for some---with higher incomes and less demanding work, while worrying about millions of job losses and increased inequality. With millions of jobs at risk of being replaced by robots, automation has become a pressing policy issue, and politicians, entrepreneurs, workers, and writers are adding to a growing discourse often labelled as "the future of work."

Consider how economists are writing about automation. MIT economist David Autor tells Kolhatkar, "It's not that we're running out of work or jobs per se \[as firms install robots\]... But a subset of people with low skill levels may not be able to earn a reasonable standard of living based on their labor." Referring to the millions of under-employed workers in the Rust Belt that voted overwhelmingly for President Trump in 2016, Autor adds, "We see that already." President Obama's 2016 economic report for Congress included a chapter on technology, with more than a dozen pages on robots. Even if the extent to which robots will replace workers is unclear, the President's report focuses on the class implications---skilled vs. unskilled, white- vs. blue-collar---of firms installing robots. The report states, "While robotics is likely to affect industrial sectors of the economy differently, it also is likely to affect occupations within these sectors differently."[^20]

Consider how technologists and software entrepreneurs speak about a future with robots at work: Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg and leading venture capitalists have suggested a universal basic income to offset the disruptive effects of robots, in which all Americans receive checks from the government.[^21] This policy solution suggests the consequences of automation are, or will be, entirely economic and similarly distributed across American families.

The framing that shapes our thinking about automation is defined by economic class concerns, rendering racial, social, and geographic inequalities absent from our conversations and policies. Neither Autor nor the President's economists acknowledge that occupations and skill levels are closely connected to, even determined by, race, education, class, criminal history, and geography. They're concerned that automation may increase inequality, admitting that the owners of capital and ideas---in other words, the entrepreneurs with patents and robots---will benefit the most, and that the distribution of capital is already highly unequal.

Even so, it seems as if the economists and politicians talking about robots are uncomfortable being explicit about the interactions between automation and racial inequality, that they'd rather not connect the dots to tell a more complete story. Raj Chetty, an influential Stanford University economist, recently found huge disparities in innovation rates by class, race, and gender. The children of wealthy parents are ten times more likely to register patents as adults than poor children, and whites are three times more likely than blacks.[^22] His research simply confirms a basic intuition: that opportunity is closely linked to wealth, and shaped by racial, class, and gender inequalities. And yet, neither economists nor politicians have explicitly linked even our basic intuitions about inequality to automation.

In many areas of the country the "low-skilled" American worker that politicians and economists like David Autor refer to is likely white. After all, nearly four out of five working Americans are white. But many of the workers in occupations most threatened by automation are non-white. In 2013, two University of Oxford professors estimated the likelihood that an occupation would be replaced by computers and robots.[^23] They did this for 702 clearly defined occupations in the U.S., and their widely-cited estimates can be matched to occupational demographic data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics.[^24]

Together, the data reveal a stark result: Occupations where black and Hispanic workers are over-represented are more likely to be automated. Black workers comprise a much larger share of certain occupations, for example in last-mile delivery drivers and as personal care aides, relative to the share of black workers in the labor force. The estimates suggest jobs in these occupations are more likely to be automated. Moreover, many of the occupations where blacks and Hispanics are severely under-represented---in medicine, engineering, and management---are hardly threatened by automation.

How we're talking about automation matters: What we say, or fail to say, reveals our social preferences and political beliefs. They affect politics and the decisions of employers. Our elected officials respond to these conversations, incorporating concerns in hearings, legislation, and guidance given to other levels and functions of government. Even so, the public discourse is clearly incomplete. The relative size of white workers in the labor force means that our conversations and public policies largely reflect their experiences. Case in point: White voters displaced by globalization and robots---from both political parties---largely shaped the policy agenda and outcome of the 2016 presidential election. But while more white Americans may lose their jobs to robots, many black and Hispanic communities face far greater disruption, and sooner. Nearly all of our conversations about automation overlook this entirely.

The same shortcoming of economists and politicians applies to roboticists, software engineers, and technology executives. Kolhatkar interviewed the director of a leading robotics lab, Stefani Tellex, who is working to make human-robot collaboration as effortless as the best interpersonal workplace interactions.[^25] Her lab's research makes it possible for Steelcase's remaining employees to work alongside robots, as machines now do both heavy lifting and precise operations. Despite the lab's collaborative focus, as director Tellex is increasingly aware of the consequences of their research advances. She acknowledges that while their research may improve the lives of some workers---as they already have for Steelcase's remaining factory employees---many low or unskilled workers may be displaced by her lab's robots. She now asks, "How can this help make society better?" when devising new machines. But the perspective through which she understands the effects of her work is entirely economic. She says, "There's enough money for everyone, I keep telling them \[her friends\]---there is. It's just not in your pocket, it's in the one per cent's pocket. If only we had the right progressive tax system, this \[automation\] wouldn't be such a problem."

Both Tellex and her Silicon Valley peers see high-level, non-targeted policies as the solution to the disruption caused by automation. Many tech workers support a universal basic income. Tellex wants a more progressive tax system. Both of these policies could certainly help low-skilled workers, but neither directly addresses the underlying economic, social, legal, and historical reasons why a black or Hispanic worker in Detroit, or Oakland, or dozens of other American cities, is especially vulnerable to the disruptive effects of automation. If anything, the widespread interest in a universal basic income throughout the left-leaning tech industry reveals the prevalence of race-, social group-, and place-blind attitudes that, more broadly, allow us to continue to ignore the inequities that underpin our economy and society today.

Journalists also contribute to this narrative: Kolhatkar's article begins with a vignette about two Steelcase employees, who started working in a Grand Rapids, Michigan metal plant in 1970s and 1980s. We learn of their privileges, how one got a job after "wait\[ing\] in line with six hundred people just to put in an application," and how the other's college dropout son managed to get and kept a job at Steelcase even as thousands of employees were laid off.

Kolhatkar ignores the race of her subjects, as if black and white workers have experienced the same outcomes as their employers install robots. The reality is African-Americans have had vastly different experiences in manufacturing than their white peers. In 1990, black and white workers were equally likely to work in manufacturing. Today, black workers are 23 percent less likely to work in manufacturing---having been laid off at faster rates than their white peers---and are less likely to be in a union.[^26]^,^[^27] Moreover, manufacturing is representative of the broader labor market. Earlier this year, when national unemployment rates reached a 17-year low, black unemployment remained nearly twice as high---and that rate of black unemployment, 6.8%, is a record low.[^28] Racial differences remain even in occupations with severe labor shortages, like construction.[^29] Furthermore, although employment gaps are decreasing, large wage gaps persist: College-educated blacks and Hispanics earn twenty percent less than their white peers.[^30]

## V. Then and Now: Approaching a tipping point

Rivera's depiction of racial equality may have been an oversight. He may have painted an integrated River Rouge to appease his patron Edsel Ford. But it may have also been his own utopian vision. Rivera sought to encourage social, political, and economic change through art; in 1931, he used a mural commission at the Pacific Stock Exchange in San Francisco to remind financiers that their wealth was possible because of laborers in agriculture and industry. In 1935, Rivera wrote, "If my work \[at the DIA\] has a purpose, it may be summed up as being to make the greatest contribution of which I am capable to the esthetic nourishment of the working class, in the form of clarifying expression of the things that class must understand in its struggle for a classless society."[^31] Thousands of Detroit's industrial workers visited the DIA to see the murals in the first year, which Rivera said in 1935 was evidence "of the worth of the decade of experimentation in art for the masses which I was just completing."[^32]

A second, more subtle, departure from reality in the murals confirms Rivera's optimism: None of the factory scenes include electric lights. Instead, daylight and light generated by machines and tools illuminate the factory and its workers. To Rivera, machines were a source of light---literally and figuratively. Flames and molten metal from the forge, energy from welding, and sparks from metal grinders light up the plant floor. That's why Rivera painted an "American Engineer" in the corner of the court that is a combined portrait of Henry Ford and Thomas Edison; he thought machines and factories like River Rouge would transform and improve society, just as the light bulb had done in the 1880s.

Perhaps surprisingly, Rivera's communist beliefs can explain the inspiration he felt after studying Detroit's capitalist factories. Germany's industrial growth and armament prior to the First World War had inspired a new social vision of work across the world---"productivism"---defined by the detailed planning and coordination of factory processes, and by the mobilization of unskilled workers. Both the political left and right offered productivism as a solution to social conflict. Industrialists like Henry Ford believed the "minute decomposition of factory labor into isolable, precise, repetitive motions" would increase production, raise profits, and lift wages.[^33] To capitalists, productivism and subsequent variants like "Fordism" were an alternative to redistribution. Many communists also believed productivism could improve living standards, while making it possible to replace industrial bosses with technocratic state officials (after a revolution, that is).[^34]

If Rivera's optimism was political, it was not unfounded, even during the Great Depression when hundreds of thousands of autoworkers were unemployed.[^35] Ford had paid many of its employees five dollars a day since 1914 (although wages fell during subsequent recessions); machines, assembly lines, and engineering improvements meant workers were dramatically more productive, and could be paid more.[^36] Detroit's "total industrial landscape," defined by thousands of firms spread out across the city, had already changed and improved the lives of millions of workers throughout the region.

Rivera's vision of a better economic future for the masses would become true a decade later. In the 1940s, manufacturing employment grew by more than 40% in Los Angeles, San Francisco, Chicago, and Detroit.[^37] After the war, Detroit was baptized as the "Arsenal of Democracy," a place where "the demand for manufacturing labor seemed boundless," and where many blue-collar workers could afford homes and cars and appliances.[^38]

Today, workers, executives, and politicians wonder if we're approaching a similar tipping point, in which robots redefine job opportunities, pay, and work responsibilities. In 2016, Congress held hearings on the "Transformative Impact of Robots and Automation," seeking an answer to a common question in the discourse surrounding the future of work. They asked, "Is this time is different?" [^39] Kolhatkar's *New Yorker* article offers evidence that the speed of technological improvements is increasing---as robots now complete simple tasks---and that robots will displace more workers than before as many of the largest employers build factories and fulfillment centers with few or no unskilled jobs.

Moreover, the economic backdrop today suggests we may be underestimating the effects of widespread change in the near future. In 1955, the same congressional committee held fifteen hearings to understand how computers and robots would advance our economy, displace workers, and change the distribution of economic growth. [^40] That report's second finding is relevant today. Congress wrote, "The shift to automation and the accelerated pace of technological change is today taking place against the background of relatively high employment levels and of a prosperous economic situation. Under such conditions, dislocations and adjustments tend to be less painful."[^41] The prosperous but increasingly unequal economy today suggests the economic and social change caused by robots in recent years has felt less painful to displaced workers, and has been less disruptive, than it may be in the future.

Rivera's Industry Murals at the Detroit Institute of Arts were a harbinger of the economic growth and modernization---but not racial equality---that much of the country experienced after the Second World War. The differences between what Rivera painted and the reality, both in the 1930s and today, confirm an unpleasant fact: that our capitalist, free-market economy has not produced racial equality. Competitive markets are supposed to be egalitarian, in that competition among buyers and sellers should expunge racial and social discrimination from economic interactions. The persistence of the racial inequalities that Rivera ignored suggests otherwise. Even periods of strong economic growth---the post-war boom, the internet fueled expansion of the 1990s, and since the Great Recession in 2008---haven't eradicated these differences; "rising tides", which many economists expect in the future as automation improves productivity, have not closed racial disparities in our economy.[^42] Our best predictions for which occupations will be replaced by robots suggests the opposite will happen in the future, as jobs in occupations overrepresented by black and Hispanic workers are more likely to be automated.

The dominant narrative about "the future of work" ignores this. We use "low- vs. high-skilled" labels instead of being clear that non-white workers may be much worse off as robots replace factory positions, and computers replace service industry and office jobs. Longstanding inequalities in labor markets may become worse as robots displace workers, alter the skills needed to be successful, and move the employment opportunities for hundreds of millions of Americans. The policy solutions coming from the discourse today---more progressive taxes, and a universal basic income---will fail to mitigate the unequal consequences of automation.

In the past, technological advancements displaced workers while creating entirely new industries and job occupations. Economists largely agree that the net effect was positive: 76% of a group of leading economists told a University of Chicago survey in 2014 that "automation has not historically reduced employment in the U.S."[^43] Workers displaced by robots will not become unemployable immediately; laid-off workers eventually will find jobs in different positions or entirely new industries. But many will face significant income shocks, both temporarily and permanently, which will affect their health, social lives, and families. Many will have to accept less desirable and stable work, in new service roles that may be replaced in the near future---such as driving for Uber. And some will stop looking for work altogether. The frequency of these outcomes will define the next tipping point in the relationship between workers and machines. But this disruption is also an opportunity, to break a cycle in which poor and non-white Americans bear the costs of economic progress. It is an opportunity to address the structural inequalities that continue to limit our progress towards Rivera's vision of a more equal society.
