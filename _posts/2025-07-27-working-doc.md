This "working document" is a collection of ideas about how I work and what's important to me.

## What I do

*My value*

I use data to solve policy and business problems for global tech teams. I help our leaders see issues more clearly, understand the options, and choose a path forward.

*My approach*

I look for issues that are pressing, high-value, and/or complex, and solve them using data, research, and modeling. I'm most excited by topics that could be actionable.

*My contributions*

Identifying and understanding an issue; finding relevant data, exploring limitations, and building pipelines / workflows to process that data; developing scenarios and models; delivering recommendations and aligning on next steps; synthesizing the above into clear and persuasive internal memos or external research, blogs, and talking points.

*My collaborators*

I work closely with Public Affairs, Legal, and Ops functions. I align with strategy and finance partners. I'll bother engineers when I need to understand our algorithms or raw data.

*Where I fit in*

I work best with a broad remit: In a RAPID decision framework, I provide input and a recommendation to a policy director, legal leader, or business GM.

## My operating principles

*I'm committed to honest, careful work*

I care deeply about getting the details right (while moving fast!) and drawing honest conclusions from data. This has been a cornerstone of Policy Research at Uber for many years. I feel lucky.

*I speak up when I make a mistake*

Whether it's a small syntax error or something bigger like misunderstanding the application of a model, errors are painful. But in the right culture, mistakes help us learn and grow. Pride in what we do and humility, together, lead to better outcomes / products / culture.

*I look for a better way*

I often solve technical problems by finding an existing solution to a similar problem. That said, I'm very aware that this can create inertia and introduce complexity. I've caught myself using a clunky, existing workflow for too long. Similarly, I've added nuts and bolts to existing functions when I should build from scratch instead. Sometimes that's OK because there isn't time. But I try to regularly ask "Is there a better way?" Clear, efficient technical work fosters clarity of thought, and wanting to find a better way is a critical input.

*I'm biased towards simple*

I start simple and iterate - with stakeholders - to add more complexity or technical rigor if it helps get us closer to solving our priorities. I'm not here to do research or to build shiny things that aren't useful.

*I value kindness*

We're all incredibly talented and ambitious. Being kind and supportive of each other should be easy and expected.

## What I value in collaboration

*Be team-oriented*

I loved the team environment of playing soccer. At work, I'm most energized by collective efforts. In both settings, however, I've experienced a key contributor or manager say "I should be [...]", or "I'm doing my job, but [...]." Even when these statements are true, it's costless to say "we," which has a powerful effect of keeping individuals and teams on track.

*Build with me*

This is corporate jargon and also a recent frustration. When someone shares an idea, make them a partner in that idea, not a participant. That means that if I send you an analysis, you leave space for us to collaborate. Unless my aim was to seed the idea, the alternative - when another org or regional team advances a shared idea in their silo - is unsatisfying and inefficient.

*Find and solve gaps*

The best cross functional teams worry less about "this is what [...] org should do." If you think there's a gap, figure out how important it is, and then speak up. This makes work more satisfying. The alternative is slow and political.

*Be constructive first, critical second*

Years ago I built a data tool for a cross-functional project team. Immediately after, an org leader sidelined the effort by publicly criticizing it - despite having little context. Experiencing this and observing other leaders has taught me the importance of seeking to understand and to build before criticizing. I've been too quick to judge someone or something more than I'd like, but strive to bring a constructive, open mindset. This feels especially important when working with more junior colleagues.

*How I like to communicate*

For a quick check-in: Slack. To clarify or align where there is nuance: Zoom. To work async: comments. To share a lot of detail: email. To share an analysis: a note. These are loosely held preferences.

*How I view success*

My definition of success is closely linked to impact. Am I working on issues that are important to the business and our users? Am I making a difference on those issues? And am I making the people around me more effective - as a teammate, org contributor, and community member?

## My areas for growth

*More focus*

I want to ask myself more frequently, "Am I working towards the most important thing I can?" I don't want every hour or day to be uniformly productive, but strive to be more focused given all of the distractions, messages, and meetings.

*More follow-up*

I spend most of my time finding opportunities, building with data, and communicating my recommendations. I need to reserve a larger share of my energy to socialize my work and for follow up. This is partly a structural issue: I'm an IC on a very small team of independent ICs. AI tooling has helped, but reorienting some of my time will grow my impact.

*Making myself more visible*

I'm a strong executor but need to broaden and deepen my audience.

*Not worrying about being more direct*

I'm working on being more direct and worrying less when I deliver criticism or a different view.
